### 6.2.2 Transport and Passenger Rights

This subsection covers the regulatory environment of the WP4 transport use cases. It is by far the most populated of the three sector subsections, because the consolidated list of regulations is dominated by instruments that govern air travel, aviation security, border controls, advance passenger information, and maritime passenger rights. The instruments addressed here apply in full or in part to UC 1 (Board the Flight), UC 2 (Electronic Travel Authorization), UC 9 (Biometric Profile for Seamless Airport Travel), Episode 1 (Smart Airport) and Episode 3 (Ferry Transport) of UC 3 (SEDIT-X), UC 4 (Streamlined Travel Experience), and, for the maritime instrument, the ferry-related parts of UC 3 and UC 4.

### Air travel and aviation security

**Directive 2001/51/EC — Carrier Sanctions Directive (supplementing the Schengen Convention).** This Directive establishes the financial liability of carriers (typically airlines) that transport passengers without valid travel documents into the Schengen area. It is the core EU reason why airlines systematically verify identity, passport validity, and, where required, visa or residence status at boarding. For WP4, the Carrier Sanctions Directive is the business-level motivation behind the identity-verification touchpoint of UC 1 (Board the Flight) and, indirectly, behind the ETA verification step of UC 2. It explains why the airline continues to perform an identity check at boarding even when other checks have already taken place earlier in the journey: the liability regime makes that last check a non-negotiable business requirement.

**Regulation (EC) No 300/2008 — Common rules in the field of civil aviation security.** This Regulation establishes the common rules governing civil aviation security in the Union. The consolidated list records its partial relevance to the credential presentation protocol, reflecting the fact that civil-aviation security processes (security checkpoints, boarding controls) constrain what a relying party at an airport can do with a wallet presentation. For UC 1, UC 3 Ep. 1, and UC 9, the Regulation sets the operational envelope within which wallet-based identity verification at the airport must fit.

**Commission Implementing Regulation (EU) 2015/1998 — Detailed measures for the implementation of aviation security standards.** This Implementing Regulation operationalises the common rules of Regulation 300/2008 by laying down detailed operational measures for airport security checkpoint and boarding processes. The consolidated list flags it as partially applicable to the credential presentation protocol and fully relevant to cybersecurity. For UC 1, UC 3 Ep. 1, and UC 9 in particular, Implementing Regulation 2015/1998 provides the operational ground rules that the wallet-based identity verification must align with at airport touchpoints.

**Regulation (EC) No 261/2004 — Air passenger rights (compensation and assistance).** This Regulation establishes the passenger rights framework for air transport, covering denied boarding, cancellations, and long delays. It is included in the consolidated list as an indication of the service-obligations context within which air-travel UCs operate; the consolidated list does not attribute direct credential-level relevance to it. For WP4, Regulation 261/2004 is a *background* instrument: a wallet-based boarding pass does not change the passenger's rights under this Regulation, but UC specifications should not assume that wallet-based boarding overrides or replaces any of the service obligations that apply at the airport.

**Spanish National Civil Aviation Security Programme.** The only national-level instrument in the consolidated list at this time is the Spanish programme adopted by *Resolución de 22 de julio de 2021*, BOE-A-2021-14195. This programme is the national operationalisation of Regulation 300/2008 and Implementing Regulation 2015/1998 for airports in Spain. Where UC 1 or UC 9 is piloted at a Spanish airport, the programme applies. Similar national programmes exist in every Member State where WP4 airports are piloted; they will be added to the consolidated list as UC leads complete the inventory.

### Border controls and advance passenger information

The next cluster of instruments applies at the intersection of air travel and the Schengen border-control framework. It is the densest regulatory cluster in the consolidated list.

**Regulation (EU) 2016/399 — Schengen Borders Code.** The Schengen Borders Code defines the rules for crossing the Union's external borders. For WP4, it is the regime under which UC 2 (ETA) operates, and it frames the external-border dimension of UC 1 when a flight crosses a Schengen external border. The Code also interacts with the DTC-based flows documented by WP3 and consumed by the WP4 air-travel UCs.

**Regulation (EU) 2017/2226 — Entry/Exit System (EES).** The Entry/Exit System regulation establishes the centralised EU-level system for recording entries and exits of third-country nationals at Schengen external borders. The consolidated list flags it as applicable to credential issuance, to credential format, and to the legal value of credentials, with data protection and cybersecurity provisions. For WP4, the EES touches UC 1 and UC 9 at the airport-border touchpoint, as well as the border-checking dimension of UC 2.

**Regulation (EU) 2018/1240 — ETIAS Regulation.** The ETIAS Regulation establishes the European Travel Information and Authorisation System, a pre-travel screening mechanism for visa-exempt third-country nationals travelling to the Schengen area. For WP4, ETIAS is the EU counterpart of the Electronic Travel Authorization that UC 2 addresses at a more general, use-case-neutral level; UC 2 uses ETIAS and similar non-EU ETAs (such as the UK ETA, see below) as the concrete instances of the travel-authorisation attestation carried in the wallet.

**Regulation (EU) 2025/12 — Advance Passenger Information for external border checks.** Adopted on 19 December 2024 and repealing Directive 2004/82/EC, this Regulation updates the regime for the collection and transfer of advance passenger information (API/APIS) by carriers to border authorities. It is directly relevant to the airline-side flows in UC 1 and to the pre-arrival steps in UC 9, because it defines what passenger data the carrier must collect and transmit, which in turn shapes the PID and DTC attributes that the airline needs to obtain from the wallet before boarding.

**Regulation (EU) 2025/13 — Advance Passenger Information for terrorism and serious crime purposes.** Adopted on the same date as Regulation (EU) 2025/12, this Regulation governs the collection and transfer of APIS data for the prevention, detection, investigation, and prosecution of terrorist offences and serious crime, amending Regulation (EU) 2019/818. It shares the same business-level motivation as Regulation 2025/12 but directs the transmission of the data to a different category of recipient. For WP4, the two Regulations act as a pair and are discussed together at the UC level.

**API and iAPI data elements.** The joint ICAO/IATA/WCO guidelines on Advance Passenger Information (API) and Interactive API (iAPI) data elements provide the technical data specification that the APIS Regulations operationalise at the carrier-to-authority interface. The consolidated list includes both the data-element guidelines and the Interactive API mechanism as operational references for the carrier-side flows of UC 1 and UC 9.

**ICAO Annex 9 — Facilitation.** ICAO Annex 9 establishes the international standards for the facilitation of civil air transport, including passenger-data facilitation and travel-document processes. It provides the international context for the API/APIS regime and for the DTC-based flows consumed by WP4.

**UK Electronic Travel Authorisation (UK ETA).** The United Kingdom's ETA scheme is an example of an administrative travel-screening system operated outside the Union. The consolidated list includes it as a concrete instance of the ETA concept that UC 2 covers in a generic way. Where a WP4 traveller holds a UK ETA issued into the wallet, the UC 2 flow specifies how the credential is presented at the destination's border touchpoint.

### Maritime transport and ferry passenger rights

**Regulation (EU) No 1177/2010 — Rights of passengers travelling by sea and inland waterway.** This Regulation establishes the passenger rights framework for maritime transport. The consolidated list records it as relevant to the legal value of credentials (partial), to data protection (partial), and to sovereignty (partial), and flags the accessibility obligations it imposes on carriers. For WP4, the Regulation applies to Episode 3 of UC 3 (Ferry Transport) and to any ferry-related leg of UC 4. As with the air passenger rights Regulation, this instrument is primarily a *background* regulation: wallet-based ferry boarding does not override the service obligations that apply under this Regulation, and the UC specifications should not assume that it does.

### Rail passenger rights: a gap in the consolidated list

The consolidated list does **not**, at the time of writing, include the EU rail passenger rights framework (Regulation (EC) No 1371/2007 and its successor, Regulation (EU) 2021/782, commonly referred to as the *rail passenger rights recast*). This is a gap that affects UC 7 (Discounted Train Fares via EUDIW) and UC 8 (Overnight Hospitality and Cross-Border Train Journey) directly, and Episode 2 of UC 3 (Urban Mobility in Athens) indirectly insofar as some of its touchpoints may involve rail operators. The gap also extends to the **Open Sales and Distribution Model (OSDM)**, which, while not a legal instrument, is the operational specification for European rail ticketing and interoperates with the legal framework.

The gap should be closed before D4.2. The corresponding item is recorded in Section 10.2 (Open Issues and Outstanding Decisions), with UC 7 and UC 8 as primary owners and UC 3 as a collaborative contributor.

### Transversal links for the transport sector

Two of the transversal instruments introduced in the Section 6.2 preamble weigh on the transport sector specifically.

* **NIS2 (Directive (EU) 2022/2555)** establishes cybersecurity obligations for essential and important entities. Air carriers, airport operators, rail operators, and certain digital infrastructure providers engaged in WP4 fall within the NIS2 perimeter. The incident-reporting and risk-management obligations of NIS2 apply to the operations of these WP4 actors, including the operations through which they interface with the EUDIW.
* **AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689)** applies to AI systems used for biometric identification. UC 9 (carrier-held biometrics for seamless airport travel) and Episode 1 of UC 3 (Smart Airport) are the two WP4 use cases most directly affected, because they involve biometric 1:1 verification at airport touchpoints. The AI Act's transparency and control obligations, together with its risk classification of biometric systems, shape the implementation of these flows.
