## Credential Types and Attestations Used in WP4

This section provides a consolidated inventory of all credential types that appear across the 11 WP4 use cases, with their data models, their issuers, and their intended verifiers. Its purpose is to fix the credential vocabulary of the pilot once, so that the per-UC subsections (7.2.1 through 7.2.11) can refer to the named credentials without redefinition.

eIDAS 2.0 (@sec-6_1) defines a small number of credential categories that together form the vocabulary of the EUDIW ecosystem. WP4 exercises four of them across its 11 use cases.

* **PID (Person Identification Data).** The foundational identity credential, mandated by Article 5b of eIDAS 2.0 and specified in detail by Implementing Regulation (EU) 2024/2977. Issued by or on behalf of a national authority of the Member State in which the wallet holder is registered, at the highest level of assurance. Carries the core identity attributes of the holder (family name, given name, date of birth, place of birth, nationality, and a Member-State-specific identifier, among others). Every WP4 use case that verifies the identity of a user relies on the PID.
* **EAA (Electronic Attestation of Attributes).** The vehicle by which sector-specific attributes are issued into the wallet and presented to relying parties. Most sector-specific WP4 credentials (boarding passes, tickets, booking confirmations, student identifiers, stadium tickets) are issued as non-qualified EAAs by private-sector credential issuers. Non-qualified EAAs carry no specific legal presumption beyond what the issuer's signature gives them.
* **QEAA (Qualified Electronic Attestation of Attributes).** A qualified variant of the EAA, issued by a qualified trust service provider under stricter rules, and carrying legal effect equivalent to a paper attestation under the Regulation. WP4 uses QEAAs in the small number of cases where qualified status is required, notably for attestations consumed by public authorities and for cross-border flows where the receiving Member State requires a qualified level of assurance.
* **DTC (Digital Travel Credential).** A passport-derived credential, defined under ICAO Doc 9303 and integrated into the EUDIW ecosystem through the work of WP3. Carries a subset of the Machine Readable Travel Document data (document number, MRZ data, biometric reference). WP4 does not issue DTCs; it consumes them at touchpoints where passport-level assurance is required (UC 1, UC 2, UC 9, and Episode 1 of UC 3).

**Data model conventions**

Every credential used in WP4 is serialised in one of two data models, in line with the protocols specified by Implementing Regulation (EU) 2024/2982.

* **mdoc (ISO/IEC 18013-5).** A CBOR-encoded, binary-friendly format originally designed for the mobile driving licence and now widely adopted for proximity flows. WP4 uses mdoc at in-person verification points where the wallet and the relying party communicate over a short-range channel: airport gates, hotel check-in kiosks, campus turnstiles, stadium entries, train carriages, and ferry boarding lanes. Well suited to offline and low-latency interactions.
* **SD-JWT VC (Selective Disclosure JSON Web Token Verifiable Credential).** A JSON-based format that supports selective disclosure natively through salted-hash commitments. WP4 uses SD-JWT VC for web-mediated and mobile-app flows where the credential is exchanged over HTTPS, such as online hotel booking (UC 10), remote ETA application (UC 2), and student-fare eligibility checks (UC 7). Integrates naturally with OpenID4VCI and OpenID4VP.

Some WP4 use cases require the same credential to be available in both data models, so that the wallet can present it both online (SD-JWT VC) and at a proximity touchpoint (mdoc). The per-UC subsections indicate, for each credential, which data models are required.

**Qualified versus non-qualified: how WP4 makes the choice**

WP4 does not treat the choice between qualified (QEAA) and non-qualified (EAA) credentials as a default, but as a design decision made for each credential on the basis of three criteria.

* **Regulatory requirement.** Where a receiving authority mandates qualified status for the attestation it consumes (for example, certain national guest-registration systems, or public services that require qualified electronic signatures), the WP4 implementation issues a QEAA.
* **Cross-border legal effect.** Where the same attestation must have equivalent legal effect in multiple Member States, the QEAA route is preferred because it removes ambiguity about the attestation's legal status.
* **Ecosystem and cost considerations.** Where an attestation is verified by a private-sector relying party that does not require qualified status (for example, a hotel verifying a booking confirmation, or a stadium verifying an event ticket), WP4 defaults to non-qualified EAAs, which are less costly to issue and still provide strong cryptographic assurance through the issuer's signature.

The per-UC subsections indicate, for each credential, which variant (EAA or QEAA) the use case intends to use during the pilot.

Sections **7.2.1** to **7.2.11** record, for each UC, the full list of credentials it relies on. Each subsection is structured uniformly into a paragraph on the **PID** (7.2.N.1) and a paragraph on the **EAAs and QEAAs** (7.2.N.2), so that PID requirements and sector-specific credentials can be compared side by side across use cases.
