#### Issuance

In **UC3: Seamless Digital Traveller Experience (SEDIT-X)**, issuance refers to the set of flows through which journey-relevant credentials are created by trusted domain actors and delivered into the traveller’s **EUDI Wallet** for later use across transport, hospitality, and destination services. In line with the overall purpose of Chapter 7, this section describes the common technical issuance pattern that underpins the WP4 use case, while episode-specific operational detail remains in Annex A. The SEDIT-X materials consistently frame issuance as a reusable wallet interaction pattern, typically aligned with **OpenID for Verifiable Credential Issuance (OpenID4VCI)** and implemented through an **EUDIW Intermediary Service** that bridges domain systems with wallet-facing issuance infrastructure.

At a general level, the SEDIT-X issuance pattern is as follows:

1. A domain system determines that a credential should be issued to the traveller.
2. The issuing system, or an intermediary acting on its behalf, creates a credential offer.
3. The traveller’s EUDI Wallet receives the offer and the traveller accepts it.
4. The wallet authenticates and requests the credential according to the applicable issuance profile.
5. The issuer returns the credential.
6. The wallet validates and stores it for later presentation.

This common issuance logic is explicitly reflected in the UC3 technical flow description and is reused across the airport, ferry, hospitality, and other SEDIT-X episodes, even where the issued credentials and operational triggers differ.

##### Common Issuance Principles Across SEDIT-X

Across the SEDIT-X use case, issuance follows a number of common principles.

First, issuance is **event-driven**: a credential is issued only when a domain process has reached the point where a trusted digital artefact should be created. Examples include successful airline check-in, successful hotel reservation creation, or completion of a ferry booking.

Second, issuance is **wallet-centric**: the resulting credential is not merely generated by a backend but is explicitly delivered into the traveller’s wallet, under the traveller’s control, for later use in presentation and verification flows.

Third, issuance is typically **mediated by the EUDIW Intermediary Service**, which reduces direct dependency between each operational system and wallet protocols. In the SEDIT-X material, this intermediary is described as the main integration layer across the airport, ferry, and hotel episodes.

Fourth, issuance is **bound to prior trusted context**. Newly issued credentials are not stand-alone tokens without provenance. They are linked to already verified identity, booking, entitlement, or travel context. For example, the airport boarding pass credential is bound to the same subject as the PID, and the hotel Booking Reference Credential is issued only after successful reservation creation.

Finally, issuance is designed to support **later selective disclosure and verification**, meaning that credentials are structured so they can be re-used at the appropriate checkpoints in the traveller journey without repeated manual data entry.

##### Issuance in the Smart Airport Episode

The airport episode contains the richest and most detailed issuance logic in the current SEDIT-X documentation. It identifies two main issuance paths relevant to the WP4 pilot.

**1. Issuance of the Boarding Pass Credential**

Before arrival at the airport, the traveller completes airline check-in, typically through the airline’s web or mobile environment. At that point, the airline issues a **Boarding Pass Credential** into the traveller’s EUDI Wallet. The airport working paper states that the passenger may first identify themselves using the PID through selective disclosure, after which the airline issues the boarding pass as a verifiable credential for storage in the wallet.

The airport material also clarifies that this credential is not meant to float independently from identity. Rather, it is **cryptographically or semantically bound to the same subject as the identity credential (PID)**, for example through a matching subject identifier or an airline-specific identifier established when the identity credential was verified. This binding is important because later airport verification steps rely on proving both flight entitlement and subject continuity.

In practical terms, the result of this issuance step is that the traveller’s wallet holds at least:

- the **PID**, already obtained from a trusted identity issuer; and
- the newly issued **Boarding Pass Credential**, representing the passenger’s flight entitlement for the specific trip.

**2. Issuance of the Biometric QR Credential**

In the airport’s **BioQRCode** path, a second airport-issued credential is created after the boarding pass has already been issued. In this variant, the traveller uses the airport mobile application or a self-service kiosk to present a combined verifiable presentation of:

- the **PID**, containing the traveller’s portrait image; and
- the **Boarding Pass Credential**.

On the basis of that prior presentation, the airport issues a **Biometric QR Credential** and stores it in the traveller’s smartphone / EUDI Wallet environment. The airport paper describes this as a digitally signed credential that contains encrypted facial data derived from the PID together with boarding-pass-related data, so that it can act as a compact journey token across airport control points.

This means that in the airport episode, issuance may be layered:

- first, the airline issues the **Boarding Pass Credential**;
- second, the airport may issue a **Biometric QR Credential** derived from prior verified evidence.

The documentation also allows for a special case where the PID does not contain a portrait image. In that case, issuance of the biometric credential may require an additional identity-proofing and biometric-enrolment step, combining PID verification, liveness checking, and proof of eligibility through a valid boarding pass. Even in that exceptional case, however, the outcome remains the same: the traveller receives a wallet-held airport credential that can later be used in checkpoint and boarding flows.

**3. Potential Issuance of Additional Airport Artefacts**

The airport material also indicates that the broader issuance infrastructure may support additional journey artefacts, such as:

- **Luggage Tag Credentials**, and
- **Proof-of-Luggage-Drop-Off Credentials**.

These are not yet the central focus of the high-level WP4 use case description, but they show that the airport episode treats issuance not as a one-off event, but as a family of operationally meaningful credential-generation steps that may progressively extend the digital journey.

##### Issuance in the Hospitality Episode

The hospitality episode provides a clean and highly reusable issuance pattern centred on the **Booking Reference Credential**.

The operational trigger is the successful creation of a hotel reservation. The hotel working paper explains that once the booking portal and downstream reservation flow have completed successfully, a **booking reference ID** is produced and returned. At that point, the booking environment triggers issuance of a **Booking Reference Credential** to the traveller’s EUDI Wallet through the **EUDIW Intermediary Service**.

The hotel materials explicitly frame this flow as a standard **OpenID4VCI 1.0** issuance scenario. The issuing function, acting on behalf of the booking portal, generates a credential offer, exposes it to the traveller’s wallet, and allows the wallet to retrieve the issued credential after any required authorization steps. The high-level sequence is:

1. a reservation is created and a booking reference ID exists;
2. the booking environment requests issuance of a Booking Reference Credential;
3. the issuer creates a credential offer;
4. the traveller opens the wallet and accepts the offer;
5. the wallet authenticates and requests the credential;
6. the issuer returns the Booking Reference Credential;
7. the wallet validates and stores it.

This credential then becomes the wallet-carried artefact that supports later hotel check-in and reservation lookup. The value of this pattern is that the reservation reference is no longer only a backend record or email confirmation; it becomes a reusable wallet credential that can be presented together with selectively disclosed identity data at the hotel.

##### Issuance in the Ferry Episode

The ferry episode is described at a somewhat higher level in the current UC3 materials, but the issuance logic is still clear. At booking time, the **ferry operator or a participating travel agency** issues a **Ferry Boarding Credential** to the traveller’s wallet. This credential then serves as the wallet-based maritime travel entitlement presented later at boarding.

The UC3 draft also indicates that, where relevant, the ferry booking and issuance flow may take into account other credentials already held by the traveller, such as:

- **student status credentials**,
- **accessibility / disability attestations**, and
- where applicable, a **Mobile Vehicle Registration Credential (mVRC)** for vehicle-related ferry travel.

In other words, the ferry issuance step is not merely ticket generation. It is capable of reflecting a verified booking and entitlement context, potentially including discount or special-travel conditions derived from already trusted wallet-held evidence.

##### Issuance in the Other SEDIT-X Episodes

The UC3 material states that the **urban mobility**, **university**, and other remaining episodes follow the same general wallet logic: credentials are issued into the wallet and are later presented to the relevant verifier. While the currently uploaded materials do not yet provide the same degree of operational detail for those episodes as for airport and hotel, the common technical pattern remains the same:

- a domain process reaches an issuance trigger;
- a credential offer is created;
- the traveller accepts the offer in the wallet;
- the issued credential is stored for later use.

##### Role of the EUDIW Intermediary Service in Issuance

A recurring design feature across the SEDIT-X material is the role of the **EUDIW Intermediary Service**. Rather than requiring every booking system, airport touchpoint, ferry operator, or hotel environment to implement wallet issuance logic independently, SEDIT-X centralises much of that complexity through an intermediary layer.

In issuance terms, this means the intermediary can:

- receive issuance triggers from operational systems,
- create or coordinate credential offers,
- expose the relevant APIs to the operational backend,
- handle wallet-facing issuance interactions, and
- reduce integration complexity across heterogeneous domain systems.

This is particularly important in SEDIT-X because the use case spans sectors with different legacy systems, varying API maturity, and different levels of readiness for direct wallet protocol integration.
