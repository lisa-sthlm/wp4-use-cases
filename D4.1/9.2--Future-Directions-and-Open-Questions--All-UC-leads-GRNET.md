## Future Directions and Open Questions (All UC leads → GRNET)

### SEDIT-X Use Case

The SEDIT-X use case is intentionally designed as a multi-domain pilot that connects transport, hospitality, payments, and academic-access scenarios through a common wallet-based interaction model. While the current specification defines the main operational direction of the pilot, several aspects will need to be refined further during implementation and piloting.

At this stage, the main future directions and open questions for UC3 can be summarised as follows.

#### Maturity of service-provider integration

Another important direction concerns the degree of technical integration that can realistically be achieved in each participating environment. Some episodes may support more advanced automation, while others may rely on partial integration or assisted operational variants.

**Open questions**
- Which participating environments can support full or near-full technical integration during the pilot?
- Where will assisted or partially manual variants remain necessary?
- How should such differences be reflected in the final pilot scope and evaluation approach?

#### Wallet-enabled payments in operational context

SEDIT-X includes payment-related interactions, notably airport excess-baggage payments and ferry-related payment flows. These interactions are important because they extend the wallet role beyond identity and entitlement verification into transaction authorisation.

**Open questions**
- What level of maturity will the WP6 payment building blocks reach in time for piloting?
- Which payment flow variants are realistic for the airport and ferry episodes?
- How should payment evidence be linked with the related operational context, such as baggage handling or ferry booking?
  
#### Evaluation of pilot success

A final future direction concerns the criteria by which SEDIT-X should be assessed. Because the use case spans multiple domains, success should not be measured only at technical level, but also in terms of usability, operational feasibility, and interoperability value.

**Open questions**
- Which indicators should be prioritised across the different episodes?
- How should cross-domain continuity be evaluated?
- How should lessons learned from partial or heterogeneous implementations be captured and reported?

#### Overall perspective

Overall, the main future direction for UC3 is to progressively move from a well-structured conceptual journey toward a realistic and testable multi-domain pilot implementation. The central open question is not whether all episodes can be made identical in technical terms, but how they can remain sufficiently aligned to demonstrate a credible seamless traveller experience based on the EUDI Wallet.

### UC6
While UC 6 successfully demonstrates the cross-border verification of student status, several areas remain open for future exploration beyond the current scope of D4.1:**
- **Long-term Credential Lifecycle Management**: currently, the pilot focuses on the credential verification, without including issuance or revocation processes. Future phases will need to address complex lifecycle scenarios, such as the automatic renewal/revocation of the European Student Card upon semester registration.
- **Extension to Additional Services**: while transportation and campus access are the primary focus, future directions include integrating the student credential with other sectors, such as museum entries, student housing, and regional healthcare services for Erasmus participants.
- **Scalability for Production Deployment**: UC6 takes place in a restricted areas, but its aim is to be interoperable in different European cities, universities and related services.


### UC7-8

UC7 and UC8 demonstrate possible interactions between the rail system and the EUDIW framework, but they can be expanded in order to include other scenarios:
- **Fully Personalized fares**: UC7 and UC8 are currenlty focused on the verification of passengers' limited attributes via EUDIW (disability and gender/age), however the integration with EUDIW could streamline the entire passenger experience, including other customizations (e.g. age-related, student-related).

### UC9
UC9 demonstrates how the EUDIW framework can improve seamless, biometric enabled airport travel at the Marseille Airport only. To further evaluate the value of the solution, several areas will require future exploration beyond the current scope of D4.1:
- **Extension to new Services**: while attributes verification within the airport before departure is the primary focus, future directions include integrating verifier systems ones the travelers arrive at destination like car rental, hotel check-in or parking discount. 
- **Extention to new Production Deployment**: UC9 takes place in a restricted area, in Marseille Airport but its aim is to be interoperable in many airports in Europe.
