### 6.2.3 Student Mobility and Academic Recognition

This subsection covers the regulatory environment of the WP4 student-mobility use cases: UC 6 (European Student Experience / Student Access via EUDI Wallet, co-led by iDAKTO and PagoPA), UC 7 (Discounted Train Fares via EUDIW, led by PagoPA), UC 11 (France-Canada Student Digital Identity, led by SIROS Foundation), and Episode 5 (Academic Access) of UC 3 (SEDIT-X, led by UAegean). It also touches UC 8 to a lesser extent, insofar as UC 8 may involve a student travelling between accommodation and a host campus.

### The main instrument in the consolidated list

At the time of writing, the consolidated list of regulations contains a single entry that is specific to this sector.

**Regulation (EU) 2021/817 establishing Erasmus+ (the Union programme for education, training, youth and sport).** This Regulation is the primary governance instrument of the Erasmus+ programme, which provides the legal framework for a large share of cross-border student mobility in the Union. The consolidated list records Regulation 2021/817 as the *main governance of student programmes*, without attributing to it direct relevance to credential issuance, protocols, format, trust models, legal value, data protection, cybersecurity, or sovereignty. This characterisation is accurate: the Regulation does not, in itself, prescribe the technical form of a student credential or the protocol by which it is verified. Its relevance to WP4 is therefore one of *context* rather than of *constraint*: it defines the programme within which the mobility takes place, the categories of eligible participants, and the institutional actors involved.

For UC 6, UC 11, and Episode 5 of UC 3, Regulation 2021/817 is the frame within which the ERASMUS dimension of the scenario exists. For UC 7, the Regulation is part of the rationale for student-fare eligibility, even though the actual fare-discount mechanism sits within the national rail operator's rule set rather than within Erasmus+.

### Instruments not yet in the consolidated list

Several instruments that one would expect to find in a student-mobility analysis are **not** present in the consolidated list at the time of writing. They are flagged here as gaps, to be closed in the late phase of T4.2 or the early phase of T4.3.

* **The European Student Card Initiative (ESCI).** ESCI is the policy and operational framework, coordinated by the European Commission and by the European Student Card Council, under which national student-card systems become interoperable across the Union. It is not, in the strict legal sense, a binding regulation, but it sets the practical framework within which a student credential issued in one Member State is accepted in another. For UC 6, UC 11, and Episode 5 of UC 3, ESCI documentation is a primary reference that the UC leads should add to the consolidated inventory.
* **ERUA Alliance governance.** The ERUA (European Reform University Alliance) operates a member-university framework under which an EUDIW-held ERUA Alliance Card entitles the holder to specific services and discounts at member universities. The governance instruments of the ERUA Alliance, and its EUDI Wallet infrastructure, are a primary operational reference for UC 6 and Episode 5 of UC 3.
* **National student-status recognition rules.** In each Member State involved in WP4 student-mobility use cases (notably Italy, France, Greece, and the non-EU Canadian dimension of UC 11), there are national rules on the recognition of student status for the purposes of transport fare discounts, campus service access, and academic credit. These rules are not uniform across Member States and are not captured in the consolidated list. UC leads (PagoPA for UC 6 and UC 7, iDAKTO for UC 6, SIROS Foundation for UC 11, UAegean for UC 3 Ep. 5) are expected to populate this part of the inventory.
* **Academic qualification recognition framework.** The Union's framework for the mutual recognition of academic qualifications (including the Lisbon Recognition Convention, the European Qualifications Framework, and the European Higher Education Area instruments) is relevant to UC 11 insofar as the France-Canada dimension of the UC touches on mutual recognition. These instruments are not in the consolidated list at the time of writing.

These gaps do not invalidate the UC specifications in Chapter 4: those specifications are grounded in operational experience of the UC leads and in the partner organisations they engage. The gaps do, however, need to be closed at the level of the **legal inventory** before D4.2, so that the implementation plan can cite a fully populated consolidated list as its legal baseline.

### Transversal links for the student-mobility sector

Two of the transversal instruments introduced in the Section 6.2 preamble weigh specifically on the student-mobility UCs.

* **GDPR (Regulation (EU) 2016/679).** The processing of student status, of academic credentials, and of fare-eligibility attributes engages the GDPR in every student-mobility UC. The purpose-limitation principle is particularly relevant: a student credential presented to a rail operator to obtain a discounted fare must not become a generic profiling attribute that the operator reuses for unrelated purposes. The UC specifications in Chapter 4 embed this constraint into the selective-disclosure field of the UC template (Section 7.4.1 and onwards).
* **PSD2 (Directive (EU) 2015/2366).** UC 6 and UC 7 include a payment step for the purchase of student-fare transport tickets. The PSD2 obligations on Strong Customer Authentication apply at these payment touchpoints. The detailed PSD2 analysis is carried by WP6 in D6.1; the UC specifications for UC 6 and UC 7 document the hand-off point from WP4 to WP6 at the payment step.

### Cross-border and non-EU considerations

UC 11 (France-Canada Student Digital Identity) is the only WP4 use case that involves a non-EU Member State (Canada) as a credential-accepting party. The consolidated list, by design, focuses on EU-level and Member-State-level instruments. The Canadian dimension of UC 11 therefore requires a separate legal inventory on the Canadian side, which is the responsibility of the SIROS Foundation as UC lead. The results of that inventory will be reflected in the UC 11 specification sheet in Annex A, and will be integrated into the consolidated list in a future revision if the UC design calls for it.

UC 6 and Episode 5 of UC 3 involve cross-border dimensions within the Union (Italy, France, Greece). The cross-border aspect is handled at two levels: at the **identity level** through the cross-border recognition obligations of eIDAS 2.0 (Section 6.1), and at the **service level** through the Erasmus+ framework discussed above. Where a specific national rule would block cross-border acceptance of a student-status attribute, the UC specification flags the item in the *Unhappy Paths* and *Open Issues* fields of the UC template (Section 3.4).

### Summary

At the level of the consolidated list, the student-mobility sector is currently represented by a single instrument (Erasmus+) and a set of transversal instruments that also apply to other sectors. The regulatory baseline for this sector is therefore **thinner** than for the transport sector documented in Section 6.2.2, but is **thicker** than the current state of the hospitality subsection (Section 6.2.1), which contains no dedicated sector instruments at this time.

The practical implication is that the student-mobility UC specifications rely more heavily on the horizontal framework of eIDAS 2.0 (Section 6.1) and on operational conventions established by ESCI and the ERUA Alliance, than on dedicated legal instruments. The gaps identified above should be closed before D4.2 so that the implementation plan can refer to a fully populated inventory. The corresponding items are recorded in Section 10.2 (Open Issues and Outstanding Decisions), with UC 6, UC 7, UC 11, and UC 3 Ep. 5 as owners.
