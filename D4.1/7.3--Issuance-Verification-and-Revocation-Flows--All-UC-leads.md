## Issuance, Verification, and Revocation Flows

This section describes the generic lifecycle patterns that apply to the credentials inventoried in @sec-7_2. Its purpose is to fix, once for the whole pilot, the three stages of every WP4 credential's life and the two transport modes through which they happen, so that the per-UC subsections (7.3.1 through 7.3.11) can refer to the named patterns without redefinition.

A WP4 credential moves through three lifecycle stages.

* **Issuance.** The attestation provider (for an EAA or QEAA) or the PID provider (for the PID) creates the credential, signs it, and delivers it into the wallet instance over OpenID4VCI. WP4 issuance flows are typically *remote*: the wallet holder requests the credential from a web or mobile-app journey hosted by the issuer (for example, a hotel chain issuing a booking confirmation, or a rail operator issuing a discounted-fare ticket). The PID issuance flow follows the same protocol pattern under the rules of Implementing Regulation (EU) 2024/2977.
* **Verification (Presentation).** The relying party requests one or more credentials, the wallet holder consents, and the wallet returns a presentation that the relying party validates cryptographically and, where required, against the trusted lists (@sec-7_5). WP4 verification flows are either *remote* (over OpenID4VP, with credentials in SD-JWT VC) or *proximity* (over ISO/IEC 18013-5, with credentials in mdoc), depending on the touchpoint.
* **Revocation.** The credential is invalidated before its natural expiry, by the issuer (for example, a cancelled booking, a refunded ticket, or a revoked student status) or by the wallet holder (for example, a lost or replaced wallet). The relying party detects the revocation through the revocation mechanism associated with the credential type (status list, accumulator, or issuer-side check), as profiled by WP2.

**Two transport modes**

The same three stages are realised through one of two transport modes.

* **Remote flows.** The wallet and the counterparty communicate over HTTPS, typically over the public internet. Used for online issuance (booking confirmations, ETA, train tickets) and for online verification (hotel pre-check-in, online ticket purchase with eligibility checks). The credential format is generally SD-JWT VC.
* **Proximity flows.** The wallet and the counterparty communicate over a short-range channel (NFC, Bluetooth Low Energy, or QR code with a local engagement step). Used for in-person verification at airport gates, hotel kiosks, campus turnstiles, stadium entries, train carriages, and ferry boarding lanes. The credential format is generally mdoc.

Some WP4 use cases combine both modes within a single end-to-end journey, with the same credential serialised in both formats. This combination is recorded in the per-UC subsections.

**Cross-cutting requirements**

Three requirements apply across all WP4 issuance, verification, and revocation flows, and are inherited by every UC.

* **Selective disclosure** at every verification touchpoint: only the attributes strictly necessary for the service are released. Detailed in @sec-7_4.
* **User consent**: every release of attributes from the wallet is explicitly authorised by the wallet holder, with the consent recorded in the wallet. Detailed in @sec-7_4.
* **Trust evaluation**: every verification step includes the cryptographic and trusted-list checks that establish whether the credential is authentic and whether its issuer is trusted. Detailed in @sec-7_5.

Sections **7.3.1** to **7.3.11** record, for each UC, the issuance, verification, and revocation flows that the use case implements.
