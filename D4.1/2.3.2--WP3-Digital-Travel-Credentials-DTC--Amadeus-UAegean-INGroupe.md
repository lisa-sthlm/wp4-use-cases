### WP3 -- Digital Travel Credentials (DTC) (Amadeus, UAegean, IN Groupe)

#### Digital Travel Credentials (DTCs) in UC3 (SEDIT-X)

**Purpose in the SEDIT-X use case**

SEDIT-X deals with Digital Travel Credentials (DTCs) in a broader context than what is defined by the ICAO-style artefact.  Specifically, SEDIT-X is based on the use of DTCs that are EUDI wallet-held, verifiable, and enable the traveller to move across the journey without repeatedly presenting paper documents. In operational terms, they are the credentials used to prove identity, travel entitlement, booking status, and access rights at the different service touchpoints that make up the Seamless Digital Traveller Experience.

In other words, in SEDIT-X DTCs are not limited to a single transport document. Instead, the term DTC is used to describe  a broader portfolio of travel-related credentials that accompany the traveller across the full journey, from airport departure and ferry boarding to hotel check-in. Their role is to support selective disclosure, stronger identity binding, cross-sector interoperability, and a more seamless user experience.

**Role of DTCs in the SEDIT-X journey**

SEDIT-X is designed as a multi-episode journey spanning aviation, urban mobility, ferry transport, hospitality, and academic access. Across these episodes, DTCs act as the reusable digital evidence that allows the traveller to prove who they are, what they are entitled to do, and which booking or journey state applies at a given moment.

More specifically, DTCs in SEDIT-X are used to:

- bind a traveller's identity to a specific journey or reservation;
- prove eligibility to proceed at operational checkpoints such as airport kiosks, bag drop, security, boarding gates, ferry boarding points, and hotel check-in terminals;
- reduce repeated manual verification by allowing the wallet to present only the attributes required for each transaction;
- support dynamic travel processes, where the state of a booking or entitlement may change during the journey; and
- provide a common credential layer across otherwise heterogeneous sectorial systems.

The importance of DTCs in SEDIT-X is not only that they digitise existing paper artefacts. Their core contribution is that they enable to reorganise the traveller-service provider interaction model around a trusted wallet and a reusable set of verifiable credentials.

This matters for four reasons.

First, DTCs allow the same traveller identity to be reused across multiple service domains without repeated manual registration.

Second, they enable selective disclosure, meaning that each relying party can receive only the data required for the relevant transaction.

Third, they improve operational continuity across sectors that traditionally operate with disconnected systems.

Fourth, they provide the basis for stronger automation at checkpoints, self-service terminals, and booking-linked processes.

**Main DTCs used in SEDIT-X**

The main credentials used for SEDIT-X  are the following:

**1. Person Identification Data (PID)**

The PID is the foundational high-assurance identity credential stored in the EUDI Wallet. It is issued by a trusted identity authority (member state) and is used as the root identity evidence throughout the SEDIT-X journey. In the airport flow, it is used to bind the traveller to the boarding pass and, depending on the path followed, may also provide the portrait image used for biometric comparison. In the hotel flow, it is used to disclose the minimum identity attributes needed for booking and check-in.

Although the PID is not a travel entitlement in itself, in SEDIT-X it functions as a core DTC enabler because all journey credentials are either issued on the basis of it or verified against it.

**2. Boarding Pass Credential (Air Transport)**

The Boarding Pass Credential is the credential in the airport episode. It is issued and is bound to the same subject as the PID. It is used during online check-in, kiosk-based check-in, luggage drop-off, security validation, and final gate boarding.

Its main purpose is to prove that the traveller has a valid entitlement for the specific flight and that this entitlement is linked to the same identity presented from the wallet.

**3. Biometric QR Code Credential**

For the airport episode, SEDIT-X in parallel to the use of the PID considers a transitional DTC in the form of an airport-issued biometric QR code credential. This credential is generated after the traveller presents their PID and boarding pass and is then used at airport control points as a compact journey token. 

**4. Ferry Boarding Credential**

For the ferry episode, the wallet stores a ferry boarding credential issued by the ferry operator or a participating travel agency. This credential is used for ticket validation, boarding, and potentially for group travel or discount-supported cases. Its role is analogous to the airport boarding pass, but within the maritime transport context.

**5. Hotel Booking / Booking Reference Credential**

For the hospitality episode, the key credential used in SEDIT-X is the hotel booking/reference credential. This credential is issued after reservation creation and stored in the traveller's wallet. Furthermore, the booking reference credential is presented during self-service hotel check-in together with the traveller's PID. Its purpose is to identify the reservation, connect the traveller to the correct booking record, and support status transitions such as reserved, checked-in, and checked-out. A distinctive feature of this credential in the SEDIT-X user journey is that it may reference a mutable booking state in the hotels IT infrastructure, allowing the booking context to evolve without necessarily re-issuing the credential itself.

**6. Luggage-related and proof-of-process credentials**

In the airport flow, SEDIT-X also foresees additional operational credentials such as a luggage tag credential and a proof-of-luggage-drop-off credential. These are not primary journey credentials in the same sense as a boarding pass, but they extend the DTC model into operational airport processes.

They are used to support baggage handling, proof of process completion, and dispute resolution if needed.

**7. Room access or stay-related credentials**

In the hospitality episode, SEDIT-X will also explore the possiblity of issuing and using room access credentials and proof-of-stay type credentials. These are follow-on DTCs that may be issued after successful identity verification and check-in and may support access to the room, hotel facilities, and later checkout or expense-related processes.

**Two presentation models for DTC use in SEDIT-X**

SEDIT-X evaluates two different ways in which DTCs can be presented:

**BioQRCode path**

In this path, the traveller first receives a biometric QR code credential and then uses that credential as the main operational token at airport checkpoints. This approach is particularly relevant where existing airport infrastructure is based on optical scanning and where a transitional integration model is needed.

**Proximity verification path**

In this path, the traveller presents a verifiable presentation directly from the wallet, typically using NFC or another proximity mechanism. The wallet presents the PID and the boarding pass credential together, allowing real-time verification without relying on a separate airport-issued QR token.

These two models are important because they show that, in SEDIT-X, DTCs are not tied to a single technical presentation method. The same travel logic can be supported either through a derived journey credential or through direct wallet-mediated presentation of the underlying credentials.

**Why DTCs matter in SEDIT-X**

DTCs in SEDIT-X should be described as the set of wallet-held verifiable travel and booking credentials that support the end-to-end traveller journey across the pilot episodes. The chapter should focus on their functional role in the use case, the main credential types involved, and the way they enable cross-sector, privacy-preserving, and interoperable journey orchestration.

Detailed protocol exchanges, API bindings, and implementation choices should remain for later technical sections or for the implementation-focused deliverable.

#### Digital Credentials in *Board the Flight* (Amadeus)

**Purpose of “Digital Credentials” in the *Board the Flight* Use Case**

In the *Board the Flight* use case, there are **2 episodes**:

Episode 1. **Collect and verify Advance Passenger Information (API)** at **Self‑Service Online Check‑In (SSCI)**, where the traveler shares a *digital credential* stored in the **EUDI Wallet**.

Episode 2. **Board the flight at the airport**, where the traveler shares a *digital credential* stored in the **EUDI Wallet**.

**Definition of “Digital Credential”**

By definition, **Digital Credentials** used in the *Board the Flight* use case refer to the following possibilities:

**1. Digital Travel Credentials (DTC)**

**Digital Travel Credentials (DTCs)**, as defined by the **ICAO‑style artefact**, are the **primary digital credentials** to be tested in the *Board the Flight* use case for both:

- **Online flow**
- **Airport (proximity) flow**

This is particularly relevant for **non‑EU flights**.

**2. Personal Identification Data (PID)**

**Personal Identification Data (PID)** represents the **authoritative digital identity of a natural person**, issued or mandated by a **Member State**.  
PID is used when a relying party needs to **identify a person**, not just verify individual attributes.

PID is planned to be tested **particularly for intra‑Schengen flights**.

**DTC and PID Relationship**

DTC and PID are complementary, not competing:

- **PID** provides a **foundational digital identity** anchored in Member State authority.
- **DTC** provides a **travel‑specific credential** optimised for aviation and border use.

In advanced travel scenarios:

- **PID** establishes *who the traveler is*
- **DTC** provides *travel‑document‑equivalent proof*

**Reference Example 1: *Helsinki–Heathrow* (Non‑EU) — DTC Testing**

Using the **Helsinki–Heathrow** journey as a reference example, the objective is to test **DTC sharing** in both:

- **Online flow**
- **Proximity flow**


For inbound travel to the United Kingdom, **airline carriers and their agents are legally required to perform passenger document and identity checks before departure**.

Under UK immigration and carrier liability regulations, airlines must:

- Verify that the traveler holds a **valid travel document** (passport or equivalent)
- **Check the identity of the traveler**
- Ensure that the **person presenting the document is its rightful holder**, including verifying that the **traveler’s facial appearance matches the portrait/photo on the identity document**


This regulatory context makes the Helsinki–Heathrow route particularly suitable for testing **DTC sharing**, as it supports:

- **Online flow**  
  Early and remote transmission of identity and travel credentials to support pre‑travel verification and compliance.

- **Proximity flow**  
  On‑site verification scenarios where an airline agent must:
  - Re‑confirm the traveler’s identity
  - Match the traveler with the portrait contained in the identity credential
  - Ensure regulatory compliance prior to boarding


WP3 is responsible for the issuance, verification, and lifecycle management of the DTC. In this example, this use case consumes the DTC during online and proximity flows

**Reference Example 2: Intra‑Schengen Flight — PID Testing**

Using an **intra‑Schengen flight from Madrid (MAD) to Frankfurt (FRA)** as a second reference example.



The Madrid–Frankfurt route is an **intra‑Schengen flight**, where passengers are **not subject to systematic identity verification during the online flow**.

However, airlines remain responsible for **identity checks during the boarding process**, in accordance with aviation security and carrier obligations.

In this context, airline agents may be required to:

- Verify the **identity of the traveler at check‑in or boarding**
- Ensure that the **person presenting the identity document is its rightful holder**
- Perform a **visual comparison between the traveler and the portrait on the identity document**, especially in manual or assisted control scenarios


Although **intra‑Schengen flights** are not subject to systematic border control,  
**Spain requires passenger identity checks during the air travel process**.

These checks are performed by airlines (and their agents) at:

- **Check‑in**, and/or
- **Boarding gates**

to ensure that the person boarding the aircraft is the passenger named on the boarding pass.

According to official guidance from **Aena (Spain’s airport operator)** and major Spanish airlines:

- The **airline is responsible** for requesting and verifying passenger identification.
- Identity checks are carried out:
  - At **check‑in**, and/or
  - At the **boarding gate**


Considering the above reasons, we decided to also test **PID** for **intra‑Schengen flights**, because **PID** should be accepted as identity check at boarding time.
