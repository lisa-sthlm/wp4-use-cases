### 6.2.1 Hospitality and Guest Registration

This subsection covers the regulatory environment of the WP4 hospitality use cases: UC 10 (Streamlined End-to-End Hotel Booking), UC 8 (Overnight Hospitality and Cross-Border Train Journey), and Episode 4 (Hospitality) of UC 3 (SEDIT-X). The scenarios addressed range from online booking through arrival, identity verification at the front desk, and, in the Member States that require it, reporting of guest data to national police-registration systems.

### Scope of the regulatory baseline

The legal environment of hotel check-in in the EU has two principal components. The first is the **horizontal identity framework**, which is already covered in Section 6.1 (eIDAS 2.0 and its Implementing Acts). Under this framework, a hotel acting as a relying party in WP4 is registered as such, limits its attribute requests to the minimum necessary for guest registration, and accepts PID attributes as presented by the wallet holder.

The second is the **transversal data-protection framework** (GDPR, Regulation (EU) 2016/679), which applies to every personal-data processing step in the hotel journey: from the booking confirmation that an online travel agency issues into the wallet, to the PID attributes presented at the front desk, to the data that the hotel transmits to a national police-reporting system where the law requires it. The GDPR implications most relevant to the hospitality scenarios are purpose limitation (the hotel must not reuse the attributes presented at check-in for marketing purposes without a separate legal basis), data minimisation (only the attributes strictly required for guest registration should be requested), and the DPIA obligation where the processing meets the Article 35 thresholds.

### National hotel-registration laws: a gap in the consolidated list

The **national guest-registration and police-reporting laws** that apply in the Member States where the WP4 hospitality use cases are piloted (notably France, Italy, and Germany for UC 10; Italy for UC 8; and Greece for UC 3 Ep. 4) are not, at this time, captured in the consolidated list of regulations maintained by the UC leads. This is a gap that needs to be closed before D4.2, because the content and reporting format of the national registration obligation directly shape the list of PID attributes that the hotel relying party must request from the wallet, the authentication assurance level it must require, and the interface it must operate towards the national police-reporting system.

The gap covers, at minimum:

* the **French** legal framework on hotel guest registration (the *fiche individuelle de police* regime);
* the **Italian** framework (police-reporting obligations administered through the *Alloggiati Web* system of the *Polizia di Stato*);
* the **German** framework (*Meldepflicht* for hotel guests under the *Bundesmeldegesetz*);
* the **Greek** framework (guest-registration obligations administered by the Hellenic Police and relevant tourism authorities).

The listing above does not represent a legal analysis. It is a placeholder for the instruments that UC 3 Ep. 4, UC 8, and UC 10 must identify and characterise during the remaining phase of T4.2. Once the instruments are identified, the corresponding national entries will be added to the consolidated list and reflected here.

### Consequences of the gap for D4.1 and next steps

For the purposes of D4.1, the consequences of the gap are twofold.

First, the **UC specifications** of UC 3 Ep. 4, UC 8, and UC 10 in Chapter 4 and in Annex A state the hospitality flow in terms of the identity attributes that the hotel will request, but they cannot yet state which of those attributes are mandated by the national registration law versus which are collected for other operational reasons. This distinction matters for the data-minimisation assessment under the GDPR, and for the qualified-or-not decision documented in Section 7.2 (for any guest-registration attestation that the hotel may need to produce as a QEAA on behalf of the guest).

Second, the **implementation plan for D4.2** will need to include a task for each affected UC lead to complete the national-law inventory, to characterise the data items that must be reported and the reporting channels and formats, and to align the PID attribute request with the minimum set that satisfies the national law. The item is added to Section 10.2 (Open Issues and Outstanding Decisions).

### Transversal considerations for hospitality scenarios

While the national specifics remain to be filled in, two transversal considerations already apply to the hospitality scenarios and are recalled here.

* **Accessibility.** The user interfaces through which wallet holders interact with the hotel (booking platform, check-in kiosk, front-desk screen) are expected to respect the applicable accessibility legislation. The relevant EU-level instruments were not captured in the consolidated list and the UC leads should confirm which national transpositions apply at each pilot site.
* **Cross-border scenarios.** UC 8 explicitly combines a cross-border train journey with a hotel stay; UC 3 Ep. 4 takes place in Greece for travellers whose PID may be issued by another Member State. In both cases, the hotel as a relying party must be able to accept a PID issued abroad, which is the core cross-border recognition obligation already anchored in Section 6.1. Where the national guest-registration law expects a specific national-ID format, the UC specification must document the mapping from the PID attributes to the format expected by the national system.

The complete regulatory picture for the hospitality sector will therefore crystallise once the national-law inventory is completed. The air- and border-related instruments that dominate the consolidated list today are the subject of the next subsection.
