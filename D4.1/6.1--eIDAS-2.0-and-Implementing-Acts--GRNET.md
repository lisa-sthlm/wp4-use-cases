## 6.1 eIDAS 2.0 and Implementing Acts

This section summarises the horizontal legal framework that applies to every WP4 use case: **Regulation (EU) 2024/1183**, commonly referred to as *eIDAS 2.0*, and the set of **Implementing Acts** adopted by the European Commission in late 2024 that give concrete effect to its provisions. The aim is not to reproduce the text of the Regulation or of the Implementing Acts, which is publicly available, but to extract the specific provisions that shape WP4 design choices and to make explicit how each provision maps to the pilot.

### From eIDAS (Regulation 910/2014) to eIDAS 2.0

The original eIDAS Regulation, adopted in 2014, established the legal basis for electronic identification and trust services in the EU: electronic signatures, seals, time stamps, registered electronic delivery services, and certificates for website authentication. It also created the mutual recognition mechanism for notified national electronic identification (eID) schemes. Under eIDAS 1.0, Member States could notify their national eID schemes, and other Member States were required to accept notified schemes at the appropriate level of assurance for access to public services.

eIDAS 2.0 revises this framework along two main axes. First, it introduces the **European Digital Identity Wallet (EUDIW)** as a new legal construct: a wallet that every Member State must make available to its citizens and that enables them to hold, manage, and present both identity attributes and electronic attestations of attributes. Second, it extends the range of regulated trust services beyond the original list to include, among others, **electronic archiving**, **electronic attestation of attributes**, and **electronic ledgers**. For WP4, the first axis is by far the most important, because the EUDIW is the technical vehicle that the pilot exercises across all 11 use cases.

The revised Regulation was adopted on 11 April 2024 and entered into force on 20 May 2024. It is directly applicable in all Member States, with a progressive activation timeline for specific provisions. The most significant deadline for WP4 is the obligation on Member States to provide at least one wallet to their citizens **by the end of 2026**, which is also the target against which APTITUDE as a whole is positioned.

### Key provisions of eIDAS 2.0 influencing WP4

Several provisions of eIDAS 2.0 shape how the WP4 use cases must be designed and piloted. The following are the ones that UC leads and implementing partners should read together with the UC specifications in Chapter 4.

**Mandatory wallet provision (Article 5a).** Each Member State is required to provide at least one EUDIW that complies with the Regulation and with the harmonised technical specifications adopted at EU level. The wallet must be offered free of charge to natural persons and, under specific conditions, to legal persons. For WP4, this means that the wallet instances used by the piloted users are, in all cases, national wallet instances produced by Member State wallet providers. WP4 does not produce a wallet of its own; it consumes what Member States make available.

**Issuance of the PID (Article 5b).** Member States are required to ensure the issuance of the Person Identification Data (PID) into the wallet, at the highest level of assurance, by or on behalf of a national authority. The PID is the foundational identity credential in the wallet, and every WP4 use case that verifies the identity of the user relies on a PID. The PID is issued under the framework of the Implementing Act on PID and rule book, discussed below.

**Electronic attestations of attributes (Articles 5a, 45a to 45g).** eIDAS 2.0 establishes the legal framework for **Electronic Attestations of Attributes (EAAs)** and for their qualified counterparts, **Qualified Electronic Attestations of Attributes (QEAAs)**. EAAs are the vehicle by which sector-specific attributes (such as boarding passes, hotel bookings, student identifiers, event tickets) are issued into the wallet and presented to relying parties. Most WP4 use cases rely on non-qualified EAAs issued by private-sector credential issuers (airlines, hotels, event organisers, student-card issuers), with the exception of certain cases in which a QEAA is needed for legal effect across borders.

**Obligations on relying parties (Article 5b, paragraphs 7 to 13).** Relying parties in the Union must accept EUDIWs when using electronic identification for access to public or private services. They must register themselves (in the manner defined by the Implementing Acts) and limit their requests to the attributes strictly necessary for the service provided. For WP4, this means that every APTITUDE partner acting as a relying party (hotel front desk, airport gate, train conductor, campus access system, stadium entry) must be registered as a relying party and must align its attribute requests with the data-minimisation principle of the Regulation.

**Cross-border recognition (Articles 6a, 6b).** The EUDIW is designed to be recognised across the entire Union. A wallet issued in one Member State must be accepted in any other Member State at the highest level of assurance for access to public services, and for private services in the cases specified by the Regulation. For WP4, cross-border recognition is a *master* requirement: several of the scenarios (for example, an Italian student travelling to a French campus with a Greek-issued PID) only make sense if cross-border recognition works at the technical and legal levels.

**Selective disclosure and unobservability (Articles 5a, 5b).** The wallet must allow its holder to disclose only the attributes that are strictly necessary for the service requested, rather than the full credential. It must also prevent the wallet provider from tracking the transactions that the holder carries out with relying parties. For WP4, these guarantees directly shape the UC specifications: every UC must include explicit selective-disclosure requirements (documented in the UC template, Section 3.4), and every interaction must be designed so that the wallet provider does not learn which relying party the user interacts with.

**Privacy by design and data protection.** eIDAS 2.0 explicitly requires that the wallet be designed to protect personal data by default, and that the processing of personal data within the EUDIW ecosystem complies with the GDPR. For WP4, this means that Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIAs) produced under WP7 inform the UC designs, and that the UC specifications must carry through the guarantees offered by the wallet (selective disclosure, unlinkability across relying parties) to the relying-party side of each interaction.

**Voluntary use and no exclusion.** The use of the EUDIW by citizens is voluntary. Relying parties must continue to accept alternative means of identification where the service historically relied on them. For WP4, this has a direct operational consequence: every piloted UC must gracefully coexist with the legacy process (paper boarding pass, physical ID at the hotel front desk, plastic student card) rather than assume that wallet-based identification is the only path available.

### The Implementing Acts adopted in late 2024

eIDAS 2.0 mandates the European Commission to adopt a set of **Implementing Acts** that provide the technical specifications needed to make the Regulation operational. The first comprehensive set of Implementing Acts was adopted on 28 November 2024 and published in the Official Journal of the European Union on 4 December 2024. The following Implementing Acts are directly relevant to WP4 and should be treated as the primary technical-legal reference for the pilot implementations:

* **Implementing Regulation (EU) 2024/2977 — Person Identification Data (PID) and rule book for the EUDIW.** Specifies the PID data model, the issuance process, the integrity and core functionalities of the EUDIW, and the rule book that PID providers and wallet providers must follow. Every WP4 UC that verifies identity relies on this Implementing Act.
* **Implementing Regulation (EU) 2024/2979 — Integrity and core functionalities of the EUDIW.** Specifies the minimum security and functional requirements that every wallet instance must meet. UC leads should treat these requirements as hard constraints on the wallet side of their UC implementations.
* **Implementing Regulation (EU) 2024/2980 — Registration and publication of trusted lists.** Specifies the machinery by which PID providers, attestation providers, and relying parties are registered and by which trusted lists are published. For WP4, this Implementing Act directly frames the trust model used by the relying parties in each UC (see Chapter 7, Section 7.5).
* **Implementing Regulation (EU) 2024/2981 — Certification of the EUDIW.** Specifies the certification scheme under which wallet instances are certified. WP4 does not produce wallets, but the pilot assumes that the wallet instances it uses have been certified or are on a path to certification.
* **Implementing Regulation (EU) 2024/2982 — Protocols and interfaces for the EUDIW.** Specifies the technical protocols (OpenID4VCI for issuance, OpenID4VP for presentation) and the interfaces that wallets, attestation providers, and relying parties must implement. UC leads should read this Implementing Act together with Chapter 7 (Technical Foundations) and the UC-specific technical flows.

Together, the Implementing Acts fill in the technical and operational detail that the Regulation leaves to secondary legislation. WP4 implementations must be **compliant with both layers**: the Regulation (which establishes the rights, the obligations, and the legal effects) and the Implementing Acts (which establish the technical rules by which those rights and obligations are actually exercised).

### Implications for WP4

Drawing the above together, five concrete implications apply to every UC specification in this deliverable and, downstream, to every implementation plan in D4.2.

1. **The wallet is a given, not a design variable.** WP4 uses the wallets produced by participating Member States in compliance with Implementing Regulation 2024/2979. UC designs must not assume capabilities that go beyond the Regulation and the Implementing Acts.
2. **PID and EAA are the only credential vehicles.** Every piece of personal information that flows from the wallet to a relying party in WP4 is either part of the PID or carried by an EAA (qualified or not) issued under the framework of Implementing Regulation 2024/2977 or the attestation rules of the Regulation itself.
3. **Data minimisation is legally mandated, not merely recommended.** Relying parties must request only the attributes that are strictly necessary. Every UC specification in Chapter 4 must include an explicit attribute list, and that list must be defensible under the data-minimisation principle.
4. **Registration as a relying party is mandatory.** Every APTITUDE partner that verifies credentials in a WP4 pilot acts as a relying party under eIDAS 2.0 and must be registered accordingly. The pilot plan in D4.2 will include a relying-party registration checklist for each partner.
5. **Cross-border recognition is not optional.** Scenarios that span Member States are tested not only for technical interoperability but also for legal acceptance across borders. Section 6.2 analyses the sector-specific rules that apply on top of the eIDAS 2.0 framework in hospitality, transport, and student mobility.
